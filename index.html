01 · the brigade

Meet the five helpers

Each one has exactly one job and one counter to work at. Tap a card to see what it actually does.

Head Chef
The Orchestrator
⌄

Takes your order and won't start cooking until it's crystal clear. Asks questions, writes it down as a proper ticket, and shows it to you before anything happens. Once you approve it, the Head Chef runs the whole kitchen on their own — you don't need to keep checking in.

agent: orchestrator · mode: primary · runs the Requirements Analysis Loop, then drives the fix/verify loop automatically via the task tool.
Grill Station
The Backend Cook
⌄

Handles everything under the hood — the logic that decides what happens when someone clicks a button, saves an order, or checks a price. Only works at their own counter and never wanders into another station's fridge.

agent: backend · mode: subagent · scoped to spring-boot-backend/ · Java / Spring Boot 3.x.
Pastry Station
The Frontend Cook
⌄

Plates everything you actually see and touch — screens, buttons, forms. Makes sure what's on the plate matches what the kitchen prepared underneath, down to the last detail.

agent: frontend · mode: subagent · scoped to angular-frontend/ · Angular 19, standalone + signals.
Larder
The Database Keeper
⌄

Organizes where everything is stored — shelves, labels, what goes next to what. Never throws anything out or restocks the walk-in without writing it down first, because everyone downstream depends on finding things exactly where they left them.

agent: database · mode: subagent · scoped to oracle-database/ · Oracle schema + versioned migrations.
Expediter
The Verifier
⌄

Stands at the pass and looks at every plate before it's allowed to leave the kitchen: does it look right, and does it match what was actually ordered? They do not taste the food — that's your job, further down. If something's off, it goes back to the exact station that made it, once. Not endlessly — once.

agent: verifier · mode: subagent · runs mvn compile / ng build only, plus a direct read of the code against the requirement's acceptance criteria. mvn test / ng test are denied at the permission level.
02 · how an order moves

From your ask to a finished plate

This is the actual order things happen in, every time. Steps in teal are the only two moments the kitchen needs you.

1
you

You place the order

Tell the Head Chef what you want, in your own words — no forms, no jargon required.

2

The Head Chef asks questions

Anything vague gets clarified before a single pan hits the stove. This step alone catches most misunderstandings.

3
you

You see the ticket before cooking starts

The Head Chef writes it all down and shows it to you. Nothing happens until you say "yes, that's right."

4

The kitchen starts on its own

From here, no more check-ins needed. The Grill, Pastry, and Larder stations get to work, each on their own counter.

5

The Expediter checks the plate

Looks right? Matches the ticket exactly? If yes, it moves to the pass. If not, one specific note gets written.

6

One fix, sent to the exact station

Only the station that made the mistake gets called back — not the whole kitchen. Then the Expediter checks again.

7

Two tries, no more

Still not right after that one fix? The kitchen stops on its own and flags exactly what's wrong — it never guesses a third time.

8
you

You taste it

A plate that "looks right" isn't the same as one that's actually good. You run it, try it, and decide for yourself — the kitchen never serves itself.

04 · kitchen vs. you

What the kitchen checks, what only you can

The Expediter is fast and thorough, but there's a line it deliberately doesn't cross.

  • ✓ The plate isn't burnt or broken — the code actually compiles/builds, no syntax errors.
  • ✓ It matches what the ticket asked for, item by item.
  • ✓ The stations' work fits together — what Pastry plates actually matches what Grill prepared.
  • ✓ It happens the instant cooking finishes, every single time, no exceptions.
  • ● Actually tasting it — running the real test suite, on purpose, only when you choose to.
  • ● Trying the finished screen yourself, clicking through it like a customer would.
  • ● Restocking the walk-in for real — running a database change against a live setup.
  • ● The final call on whether it ships. The kitchen never decides that for you.
05 · house rules

Why the kitchen can't go off the rails

Three simple rules keep an unsupervised kitchen from making a mess.

01 Every station stays at its own counter

The Grill can't reach into the Larder's shelves, and the Larder can't touch Pastry's plates. This isn't a polite request — the kitchen is physically built so it's not possible.

02 Two tries, then it stops and asks

An unattended kitchen that keeps guessing forever is a liability. After one fix attempt, if it's still not right, it stops and tells you exactly what's wrong instead of trying a third time.

03 The kitchen never tastes its own food

Checking that a plate "looks correct" and confirming it's genuinely good are different jobs on purpose. The first is fast and automatic; the second needs a human, every time.

08 · context engineering

        
How the kitchen remembers anything at all

        
Here's the part that makes the whole thing work: none of the cooks have a memory. Every time a station is called in, it's like their very first shift. Context engineering is just a fancy name for the logbook system that lets a brand-new shift pick up exactly where the last one left off.

      


      

        

          
the standing recipe book

          
memory.md

          
Decisions that don't change often — naming conventions, house style, lessons learned the hard way. A cook reads this once and mostly won't need to touch it again.

          
Updated rarely. Kept lean on purpose — each station's AGENTS.md says to fold it down once it passes ~150 lines so it doesn't drown out what matters.

        

        

          
today's prep list

          
plan.md

          
What this station is working toward right now. The Head Chef writes this the moment an order is approved, so every station knows its job before it even starts cooking.

          
Written by the orchestrator during Step 5 of the Requirements Analysis Loop, per affected module.

        

        

          
the shift-change notepad

          
state.md

          
Exactly where the last shift left off — what's done, what's half-finished, what the next cook needs to know before touching anything. Updated at the end of every single shift, no exceptions.

          
Every agent persona ends with "update .context/state.md at the end of every session" — this is the mechanism, not a suggestion.

        

      


      

        
station's own binder+shared corkboard→what a cook reads before lifting a finger

      


      

        
Why every station also checks the shared corkboard

        
A station's own binder only covers its own counter. But Pastry needs to know what shape of dish Grill is sending over, and Grill needs to know what the Larder actually has in stock. shared-context/ is the corkboard in the middle of the kitchen — any station can read it, and anything that crosses counters gets pinned there instead of shouted across the room.

        
Cross-module contracts (API shapes, schema changes) live in shared-context/memory.md and shared-context/state.md, read by every subagent alongside its own .context/.

      


      

        
Why this matters more than it sounds like it should

        
When the Head Chef calls in the Grill Station, that cook shows up with a completely blank memory of every previous order this kitchen has ever cooked. Without a binder to read, they'd have to be told everything from scratch, every single time — or worse, guess. The binder is the entire reason a kitchen with no memory can still run consistently, order after order.

        
Each subagent invocation is a fresh child session — it does not automatically inherit the orchestrator's conversation history. The .context/ files are what make that statelessness harmless instead of costly.
